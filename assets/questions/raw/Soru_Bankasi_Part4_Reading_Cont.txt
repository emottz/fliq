--- PASSAGE 31: HYDRAULIC ACCUMULATOR ---

The hydraulic accumulator is a pressure storage device in an aircraft hydraulic system. It consists of a sealed cylinder divided into two chambers by a flexible diaphragm or a free-floating piston. One chamber is pre-charged with dry nitrogen gas, and the other chamber is connected to the hydraulic system. When hydraulic pressure increases, fluid compresses the nitrogen charge. The stored pressure energy can then be used to supply instantaneous high-pressure fluid to operate actuators, smooth out pressure fluctuations, and provide an emergency backup source of hydraulic pressure if the pump fails. The nitrogen pre-charge pressure must be checked at specified intervals using a calibrated nitrogen charging kit, with the hydraulic system fully depressurized. If the pre-charge is low, the accumulator's capacity is reduced; if it is too high, the accumulator cannot accept fluid from the system and becomes ineffective.

--- PASSAGE 32: ENGINE OIL SYSTEM ---

The engine lubrication system provides clean, cooled oil to all moving parts of the engine to reduce friction and wear, remove heat, and prevent corrosion. The system typically comprises an oil tank, a pressure pump, a scavenge pump, filters, and an oil cooler. Oil is drawn from the tank by the pressure pump and delivered at regulated pressure through filters to the engine bearings, gearbox, and other components. After passing through the engine, the scavenge pump returns the oil to the tank via the oil cooler, where excess heat is transferred to fuel or air. An oil quantity check is performed within a specified time after engine shutdown to allow oil to drain back to the tank. Consumption is monitored and compared against the aircraft manufacturer's specification. Oil that is contaminated, discoloured, or contains metallic particles should be sampled and sent for analysis to identify the source of the contamination.

--- PASSAGE 33: AIRCRAFT ELECTRICAL GENERATION ---

Aircraft electrical power is generated by alternators or generators driven by the engines and, on many modern aircraft, by a dedicated auxiliary power unit (APU) generator. AC (alternating current) systems are more common on large transport aircraft because AC power can be transformed to different voltages and is more efficient for long cable runs. The main generators produce AC power at a constant frequency of 400 Hz, regulated by integrated drive generators (IDGs) or constant speed drives (CSDs). DC (direct current) power is produced by transformer rectifier units (TRUs) from the AC bus and is used for avionics, batteries, and other DC consumers. In the event of all generator failures, the aircraft can extend a ram air turbine (RAT) that uses airstream energy to generate emergency hydraulic or electrical power. Battery power provides a final backup, though it is limited in capacity and duration.

--- PASSAGE 34: AVIONICS COOLING ---

Avionics equipment generates heat during operation and requires effective cooling to maintain reliable performance and prevent premature failure. Modern aircraft use dedicated avionics cooling systems that circulate conditioned air through the avionics bays. The conditioned air is supplied from the aircraft's pneumatic or environmental control system, passes through the avionics bay, and is either exhausted overboard or mixed with cabin air. The flow rate of cooling air is monitored and, if it falls below a threshold, a cooling fault is annunciated on the master caution panel. On the ground, cooling may be supplemented by ground cooling units connected to the aircraft during maintenance when avionics are powered up for testing. Technicians must ensure that all avionics cooling ducting and flow sensors are reconnected correctly after maintenance, as incorrect reassembly can lead to inadequate cooling and equipment failure.

--- PASSAGE 35: WING ANTI-ICING SYSTEM ---

The wing leading edge anti-icing system on many transport aircraft uses hot bleed air from the engine compressor to heat the leading edge slats. This prevents the accumulation of ice, which would alter the wing's aerodynamic profile and reduce lift. The bleed air is distributed through piccolo tubes running along the leading edge, which have multiple small holes allowing the hot air to impinge on the inner surface of the slat. On some aircraft, the anti-icing system is fully automatic and activates when the ice detection system senses icing conditions. On others, the crew selects the system manually. The anti-icing system must be tested before flight in forecast icing conditions. During maintenance, after any work on the bleed air supply or piccolo tube system, a leak check and functional test must be performed. Damage to the leading edge such as dents or cracks must be assessed against the structural repair manual to determine acceptability.

--- PASSAGE 36: MAINTENANCE ERROR AND HUMAN FACTORS ---

Maintenance errors are a significant contributor to aviation accidents and incidents. Research into human factors in maintenance has identified twelve common conditions that can lead to errors, often referred to as the "Dirty Dozen". These include lack of communication, complacency, lack of knowledge, distraction, lack of teamwork, fatigue, lack of resources, pressure, lack of assertiveness, stress, lack of awareness, and norms (unwritten rules). Fatigue is particularly dangerous as it reduces concentration, slows reaction times, and impairs decision-making. Organisations address these risks through training, procedural discipline, checklists, and a just culture that encourages error reporting without fear of punishment. Post-maintenance inspections by a second inspector are a critical control, especially for tasks involving critical systems. The goal is not simply to avoid blame, but to understand why errors occur and to put systemic defences in place to prevent recurrence.

--- PASSAGE 37: FUEL SYSTEM CONTAMINATION ---

Fuel contamination is a major concern in aircraft operations and maintenance. Common contaminants include water, microbiological growth (fungi and bacteria), sediment, and mixing of incompatible fuel types. Water can enter the fuel tanks through condensation as the aircraft descends into warmer humid air, through poorly sealed filler caps, or through contaminated refuelling equipment. Water in aviation fuel can cause fuel system icing in the delivery lines at altitude and can support microbiological growth on the tank surfaces. Microbiological contamination produces acids, dark sediment, and can cause corrosion to tank structure and fuel system components. Regular tank sump checks, in which the lowest point of the tank is drained and the sample inspected for water and debris, are a primary defence. Biocide additives may be used in some aircraft to control microbial growth. Fuel contamination by incorrect fuel type is extremely serious and can cause engine damage or failure.

--- PASSAGE 38: STRUCTURAL INSPECTION METHODS ---

Aircraft structures are inspected using a variety of methods depending on the type of material and the type of defect being sought. Visual inspection is the most basic method and is used to identify obvious damage, corrosion, and loose fasteners. Dye penetrant inspection (DPI) is used to find surface-breaking cracks in non-porous materials such as aluminium and stainless steel. The component is cleaned, penetrant fluid is applied, excess is removed, and developer is applied to draw out the penetrant from any cracks, making them visible. Magnetic particle inspection (MPI) is used on ferrous (magnetic) metals and reveals both surface and near-surface defects using a magnetic field and iron particles. Ultrasonic testing sends high-frequency sound waves through the material; defects reflect the waves back, creating indications on the display. Eddy current testing uses electromagnetic induction to detect cracks in conductive materials and is particularly effective for detecting corrosion under aircraft skin. X-ray testing creates an image of the internal structure and is used to inspect complex assemblies, welds, and areas where other methods cannot access.

--- PASSAGE 39: BRAKE SYSTEM MAINTENANCE ---

The aircraft brake system provides deceleration during landing, directional control during ground manoeuvring, and parking. Most large aircraft use multiple disc brakes with hydraulically actuated pistons that press the brake discs together when the pilot applies pedal pressure. Carbon fibre composite (CFC) brake discs are used on many modern aircraft because they are lighter than steel and can withstand higher operating temperatures. However, carbon discs must be handled carefully as they are brittle and can crack if dropped or subjected to impact. Carbon disc performance also degrades if they become contaminated with oil, grease, or water. At each maintenance check, brake wear must be measured against the wear indicator pins or disc wear limits in the maintenance manual. When wear exceeds limits, the brake assembly must be replaced. The anti-skid system prevents wheel lockup by modulating hydraulic pressure to each brake if wheel speed drops below a threshold related to aircraft speed.

--- PASSAGE 40: AIRCRAFT GROUND HANDLING ---

Aircraft ground handling encompasses all activities on the ground from the time an aircraft arrives until it departs. This includes aircraft marshalling, ground power connection, aircraft chocking, fuelling, catering, loading, pushback, and towing. Ground handling must be performed by trained and authorised personnel to prevent damage to the aircraft or injury to personnel. When connecting ground power to an aircraft, the power source must be verified as compatible with the aircraft's electrical system requirements before connection. During fuelling, the aircraft must be bonded to earth to dissipate static charges. The maximum towing speed must not be exceeded, and the aircraft's nose gear must not be turned beyond its stated steering angle limits. During pushback, a qualified operator must ensure clear communication with the flight crew before and during the manoeuvre. After pushback, the towbar must be correctly disconnected and the aircraft's nose wheel steering reconnected before releasing the aircraft to the crew.

--- PASSAGE 41: ENGINE VIBRATION MONITORING ---

Engine vibration monitoring (EVM) is a condition monitoring technique used to detect developing mechanical faults in aircraft engines. Sensors called accelerometers are mounted on the engine case and measure vibration levels in units called IPS (inches per second) or similar. The readings are displayed in the cockpit or recorded by the engine health monitoring system. An increase in vibration level may indicate fan or turbine blade damage, bearing wear, rotor imbalance, or other mechanical deterioration. When vibration levels exceed alert thresholds, maintenance action is required, which may include a borescope inspection of the fan or turbine stages, oil sampling and analysis for metal particles, or rotor trim balancing. Rotor trim balancing corrects small rotor imbalances by adding or removing small balance weights at designated positions on the fan or compressor rotor. Regular trending of vibration data helps maintenance engineers identify gradual deterioration before it reaches a critical level.

--- PASSAGE 42: WEIGHT AND BALANCE FUNDAMENTALS ---

Weight and balance management is critical to the safe operation of any aircraft. The maximum certificated weights (maximum takeoff weight, maximum landing weight, maximum zero fuel weight) must never be exceeded, as exceeding them can stress structural components beyond their design limits. The centre of gravity (CG) must remain within the forward and aft limits throughout the flight, as a CG outside these limits can make the aircraft uncontrollable or structurally unsafe. The basic empty weight of the aircraft is established at manufacture and confirmed after any modification or major structural repair. This weight, combined with the weights of fuel, crew, passengers, and cargo, gives the total weight. The arm (distance from a reference datum to each weight item) and moment (weight × arm) calculations determine the CG position. Weight and balance calculations must be completed and verified before every flight, and are typically documented on a load sheet that accompanies the aircraft.

--- PASSAGE 43: AIRCRAFT WASHING AND CORROSION PREVENTION ---

Regular aircraft washing is an important part of a corrosion prevention programme. Accumulated dirt, exhaust deposits, road salt, and industrial pollutants on the aircraft skin create conditions that promote corrosion, especially in dissimilar metal joints and crevice areas. Washing procedures are specified in the aircraft maintenance manual and typically use approved aircraft washing compounds and clean water. High-pressure water jets must not be directed at seals, vents, pressure-sensitive instruments, or into control surface hinge gaps. After washing, the aircraft must be dried to prevent water pooling and checked to ensure all drain holes are clear. Corrosion inhibiting compounds (CICs) are applied to specified internal structural areas to provide a protective film. Any corrosion found during washing or subsequent inspection must be assessed against the structural repair manual to determine whether it is within acceptable limits or requires treatment or structural repair.

--- PASSAGE 44: OXYGEN SYSTEM TYPES ---

Aircraft oxygen systems are designed to supply oxygen to crew and passengers at high altitude where ambient oxygen partial pressure is insufficient for normal physiological function. There are three main types of oxygen systems used in aviation: gaseous oxygen systems using high-pressure cylinders, chemical oxygen generators (solid state), and liquid oxygen systems. Gaseous systems store oxygen at high pressure (typically 1850 psi) in green cylinders. When activated, a regulator reduces the pressure for delivery to masks. Chemical oxygen generators produce oxygen by a chemical reaction initiated by a firing pin mechanism when the passenger pulls the mask lanyard. These are used in passenger overhead panels because they are lightweight and require no high-pressure plumbing. Liquid oxygen (LOX) systems are used in military aircraft; liquid oxygen is converted to gaseous oxygen by passing through a heat exchanger before delivery. All oxygen system components must be kept completely free of oil and grease, as these materials can ignite violently on contact with high-pressure oxygen.

--- PASSAGE 46: LANDING GEAR RETRACTION SYSTEM ---

The landing gear retraction and extension system operates the landing gear between the extended (down and locked) and retracted (up and locked) positions. The system uses hydraulic actuators to drive the gear and gear doors. Position is sensed by proximity switches or microswitches that signal the cockpit indicator lights. In the extended position, the gear is held by mechanical downlocks that prevent collapse under load. In the retracted position, uplocks secure the gear in the wheel well. The normal retraction sequence is: gear doors open, gear retracts, gear doors close. On extension: gear doors open, gear extends and locks down, gear doors close (some aircraft doors remain open when gear is down). In the event of hydraulic failure, most aircraft provide an emergency extension system that uses either hydraulic pressure from an alternative source, pneumatic pressure, or gravity (free-fall) to lower the gear. Retraction tests on the ground must only be performed with the aircraft on jacks and the wheel well area clear.

--- PASSAGE 47: CORROSION TYPES AND TREATMENT ---

Corrosion is the deterioration of a material due to chemical or electrochemical reaction with its environment. In aircraft maintenance, several types of corrosion are encountered. Uniform attack corrosion occurs over a broad area. Pitting corrosion forms small, deep holes that can be more damaging than they appear. Intergranular corrosion occurs along the grain boundaries of an alloy, particularly in certain aluminium alloys, and can seriously weaken a structure while being invisible from the surface. Filiform corrosion appears as a network of worm-like threads under paint or other coatings. Galvanic corrosion occurs when two dissimilar metals are in electrical contact in the presence of an electrolyte. Exfoliation corrosion is a type of intergranular corrosion that causes layers to peel away. Fretting corrosion results from the micro-motion between closely fitted parts. Treatment involves removal of the corrosion by mechanical or chemical means, assessment against corrosion limits, application of a conversion coating, primer, and topcoat.

--- PASSAGE 48: FUEL QUANTITY INDICATING SYSTEM ---

The aircraft fuel quantity indicating system (FQIS) measures the fuel load in each tank and displays it on cockpit indicators. Most modern systems use capacitance probes located inside the fuel tanks. The electrical capacitance of the probe changes as the fuel level rises and falls, since fuel has a different dielectric constant than air. This change in capacitance is used to calculate fuel quantity, corrected for fuel density (temperature compensated). A totaliser shows the total fuel quantity across all tanks. In addition to the main capacitance system, some aircraft have float-type mechanical gauges accessible through access panels in the wing to confirm minimum fuel quantity. FQIS accuracy depends on probe cleanliness and correct calibration. Maintenance tasks include probe resistance checks, leak checks at probe connections, and system functional tests to verify calibration across the full range. If a probe indicates out of range, it may be faulty or affected by fuel contamination.

--- PASSAGE 49: PRESSURIZATION SYSTEM MAINTENANCE ---

The aircraft pressurization system maintains the cabin at a comfortable pressure altitude even as the aircraft climbs to cruising altitude. The system uses engine bleed air or dedicated electric compressors to supply conditioned air to the cabin. Outflow valves, controlled automatically or manually, regulate the rate at which air is vented overboard to maintain the desired cabin pressure differential. Safety relief valves protect the fuselage from overpressurisation by opening if the differential pressure exceeds the maximum design limit. Negative pressure relief valves prevent outside air pressure from exceeding cabin pressure during descent. Maintenance tasks include inspection of door seals and window seals, functional tests of the outflow and safety relief valves, and checking the condition and setting of the cabin pressure controller. Cabin altitude and differential pressure warnings must be tested to confirm they alert the crew at the correct thresholds. A pressure differential test of the aircraft fuselage structure is performed periodically to verify structural integrity.

--- PASSAGE 50: TURBINE ENGINE INSPECTION AFTER EXCEEDANCE ---

--- PASSAGE 52: HYDRAULIC FILTER MAINTENANCE ---

Hydraulic filters remove contamination from hydraulic fluid to protect system components from wear and blockage. After filter replacement, the system must be operated and the filter area checked for leaks.

--- PASSAGE 53: AIRCRAFT FIRE PROTECTION SYSTEM ---

The aircraft fire protection system is designed to detect and suppress fires in the engine nacelles, APU bay, and cargo compartments. Fire detection loops consisting of sensing elements are routed around each protected zone. When a fire or overheat condition is detected, a warning light and aural alert are triggered in the cockpit. The pilot initiates fire suppression by pressing the engine fire push button, which isolates the engine from fuel, hydraulics, and electrical systems, and arms the fire extinguisher. Most large aircraft have two extinguisher bottles per engine to provide a second shot if the first does not extinguish the fire. The fire suppression agent quantity (pressure) in each bottle must be verified before every flight and the detection loop tested at each maintenance check. Cargo compartment fire detection relies on smoke detectors, and cargo fire suppression uses a lower concentration agent delivered over time to deplete oxygen levels.

--- PASSAGE 54: COMPOSITE STRUCTURE DAMAGE ASSESSMENT ---

--- PASSAGE 58: APU (AUXILIARY POWER UNIT) MAINTENANCE ---

The Auxiliary Power Unit (APU) is a small gas turbine engine typically located in the tail cone of the aircraft. Corrosion in aircraft fuel tanks is a significant maintenance concern and can compromise structural integrity and fuel system reliability. Deep pitting may require structural repair or panel replacement. Corrosion inhibitors added to the fuel also help to reduce the rate of corrosion.

PASSAGE 72: AIRCRAFT FLIGHT CONTROL SYSTEMS

Modern aircraft use either mechanical, fly-by-wire, or hybrid flight control systems to translate pilot inputs into control surface movements. In mechanical systems, pilot inputs are transmitted through cables, pulleys, and rods to move the control surfaces. These systems require regular rigging checks to maintain correct control surface positions and movement ranges. Fly-by-wire (FBW) systems replace most mechanical linkages with electrical wiring, with computers processing pilot inputs and commanding actuators. FBW systems provide envelope protection, preventing the pilot from commanding movements that exceed the aircraft's structural or aerodynamic limits. Maintenance of FBW systems involves functional testing of sensors, computers, and actuators, as well as software version verification. After any work on flight control systems — mechanical or FBW — a full range of movement check must be performed to verify correct operation before return to service.

PASSAGE 73: AIRCRAFT PNEUMATIC SYSTEMS

Aircraft pneumatic systems use compressed air as a working fluid for a range of functions. On most large jet aircraft, bleed air from the engine compressor stages is the primary source of pneumatic pressure. This bleed air, after pressure regulation and temperature conditioning, is used for cabin air supply, wing anti-icing, engine starting, thrust reversers, and other actuators. Precoolers reduce the temperature of bleed air before it enters the pneumatic manifold. Pressure regulating and shutoff valves (PRSOVs) control the pressure and flow of bleed air. If bleed air temperature or pressure exceeds limits, the overheat and overpressure protection systems close the supply valves to protect the distribution system. Maintenance includes inspection of bleed air ducts for hot air leaks, testing of temperature sensors and PRSOVs, and checking duct couplings and clamps. Hot air leaks in the bleed air system can cause significant structural damage if undetected.

PASSAGE 74: SAFETY MANAGEMENT IN MAINTENANCE

A Safety Management System (SMS) in a maintenance organisation provides a systematic approach to identifying, assessing, and mitigating safety risks. The four pillars of SMS are: safety policy, safety risk management, safety assurance, and safety promotion. Safety risk management involves hazard identification, risk assessment, and the implementation of risk controls. Safety assurance involves monitoring and measuring safety performance to ensure that controls remain effective. Hazard reports submitted by maintenance personnel are a key input to the SMS. An open reporting culture where personnel report errors and near-misses without fear of punishment is essential for an effective SMS. PASSAGE 75: AIRCRAFT FUEL TYPES AND SPECIFICATIONS

Aviation fuels must meet strict specifications to ensure safe, reliable engine operation across the operating range. Jet A and Jet A-1 are the most widely used turbine fuels in commercial aviation. Jet A-1 has a lower freeze point (-47°C) than Jet A (-40°C), making it preferred for long-range international operations where aircraft may be exposed to extreme cold. Jet B is a wider-cut fuel used in some military and cold-weather operations. AVGAS (aviation gasoline) is used in piston-engine aircraft. Jet fuels must meet specifications for freeze point, flash point, density, viscosity, thermal stability, and lubricity. Misfuelling (filling a turbine aircraft with AVGAS or vice versa) is extremely dangerous. Before fuelling, the fuel type, batch number, and quality documentation must be checked by the fuelling operator. Fuel uplifted must be recorded in the technical log.
